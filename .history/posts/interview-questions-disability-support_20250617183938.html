The Most Common Interview Questions for Disability Support Workers

Published: 17 June 2025

Preparing for a job interview as a disability support worker? Whether you're new to the field or looking to advance your career, knowing what to expect can help you feel more confident and ready to impress. 1. Why do you want to work as a disability support worker?

This question is designed to gauge your motivation and values. Employers want to know that you're committed to making a difference and not just looking for any job.

Tip: Share a personal story or passion that connects you to the care sector, and highlight your desire to help people live more independently and with dignity.

2. How would you handle a situation where a client is becoming agitated or distressed?

This tests your emotional intelligence and problem-solving skills. Interviewers want to know that you can stay calm and respond appropriately under pressure.

Tip: Explain how you'd use communication techniques, give the person space if needed, and follow any behaviour support plans in place. Always emphasise respect and safety.

3. Can you describe a time when you worked as part of a team?

Support work often involves collaboration with families, other carers, or allied health professionals. This question checks your teamwork and communication skills.

Tip: Use the STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to describe a real-life example where you worked effectively with others to support a client.

4. What do you understand about person-centred care?

This is a key principle in disability support. Employers want to know you prioritise the individual's needs, choices, and goals.

Tip: Explain that person-centred care means tailoring your support to each person, involving them in decisions, and helping them build independence and confidence.

5. Do you have experience with manual handling or using mobility aids?

If the role involves physical support, the employer needs to know you're trained or comfortable with these tasks.

Tip: If you have formal training, mention it. If not, express a willingness to learn and follow proper procedures for safety.

Final Thoughts

Interviews can be nerve-wracking, but being prepared makes all the difference. Practise your answers, stay calm, and remember that authenticity matters. The best disability support workers lead with empathy, patience, and a genuine desire to help.
