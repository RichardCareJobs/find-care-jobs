Understanding the NDIS and What It Means for Disability Support Workers

Published: 14 June 2025

The National Disability Insurance Scheme (NDIS) has transformed the way disability services are funded and delivered in Australia. For disability support workers, it has opened up new opportunities, introduced new responsibilities, and created a more client-centred approach to care. Whether you’re new to the sector or looking to better understand your role under the NDIS, here’s what you need to know.

What Is the NDIS?

The NDIS is a government-funded scheme that provides support for Australians with a permanent and significant disability. It’s designed to help people live more independently, access the community, gain employment, and improve their overall quality of life. Unlike previous models of care, the NDIS gives participants more choice and control over the services they receive and who provides them.

As of 2025, the scheme supports over 600,000 Australians and continues to expand its reach across regional and remote communities. This growth means more demand for qualified and compassionate disability support workers than ever before.

What Does This Mean for Support Workers?

For disability support workers, the NDIS brings both opportunities and changes. Here are some of the key impacts:

  • Greater Flexibility: Many workers are now employed by smaller providers or work directly with participants as independent contractors. This flexibility can offer more control over your hours, but it also means managing your own schedule and administration.
  • Increased Variety: Job roles can vary depending on each participant’s individual plan. One day you might be assisting with personal care, and the next, supporting someone at their workplace or helping them attend a social event.
  • Participant-Centred Approach: The NDIS prioritises the goals and preferences of the participant. Support workers are expected to work collaboratively with individuals and their families to help achieve those goals.
  • Documentation and Reporting: Accurate record keeping is more important than ever. Workers often need to complete shift notes or progress reports, particularly when working for NDIS-registered providers.

How to Get Started or Upskill

If you’re considering a role in the disability sector, a Certificate III in Individual Support (Disability) is a common entry point. There are also many short courses and online programs to help workers understand NDIS processes, rights, and responsibilities.

It's also helpful to understand the NDIS Code of Conduct and Worker Screening Check, especially if you plan to work with registered providers or directly with participants.

Final Thoughts

The NDIS has changed the landscape of disability support work in Australia. While it brings more responsibility in terms of accountability and flexibility, it also allows support workers to have a real, meaningful impact on people’s lives. If you’re someone who values purpose-driven work, the NDIS offers a rewarding and evolving career path.
